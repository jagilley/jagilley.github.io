AI-native technical interviewing

Jasper Gilley
San Francisco, California
April 2025

It’s no secret that technical interviews have always been a mediocre-to-bad proxy for actual software engineering skills:

*points to the sign*

And it’s no secret either that whatever signal whiteboard/Leetcode-style interviews had is degrading quickly now that even small LLMs can zero-shot these problems.

There is a solution, if you have the resources and desire to administer it. The top echelon of startups (OpenAI, Stripe, Coinbase, etc.) has moved towards open-ended take-home problems that more closely mirror the day-to-day of an actual engineering job. But there are a few problems with take-homes:

  1. Not many candidates complete them (maybe as low as 20%) unless you’re a brand name company
  2. They take a long time for candidates to complete
  3. Open-ended problems are difficult to grade by nature
  4. They don’t scale well with number of applicants
  5. You can still prompt AI to do it for you, sort of

I think these problems were mostly intractable until the release of o3 a few weeks ago, which to me qualitatively feels like the first model capable of both designing and evaluating open-ended take-homes in a truly sophisticated manner.

Here’s what I think AI-native technical interviewing looks like:

  1. Generate a customized technical challenge with o3. It can be specific to the company and its branding, specific to the role, specific to the candidate’s background, etc. Provide a little scaffolding, but leave the problem deliberately open-ended for the candidate to run with.
  2. Encourage the candidate to use all tools at their disposal, including AI, to solve the problem
  3. Evaluate the candidate’s solution with o3. Generate specific questions that test how deeply they know their own solution. If you must select for Leetcode-style data structures & algorithms knowledge, generate a specific augmentation of their solution that requires such knowledge.
  4. Have the candidate walk through their solution on a live call. Optionally, have them implement the proposed DS&A augmentation to their solution. Candidates who truly grok their own code can pivot in real time; those who cargo-culted GPT output cannot
  5. Present the final solution to the hiring manager. If you design the tech challenge well enough, you can nudge the candidate towards a “demo-able” solution that stands on its own.

I think this solution solves most of the problems affiliated with traditional take-homes:

  • Problem 1: low completion rates can be alleviated by making the technical challenge genuinely exciting enough that great candidates get nerdsniped by it
  • Problem 2: long completion times becomes a non-factor for candidates who are sufficiently adept at using AI coding tools. It’s hard to imagine a technical challenge that you couldn’t make very meaningful progress on in an hour or two
  • Problem 3: difficulty in grading is almost completely alleviated by automated AI evaluation along specific axes (e.g., maintainability, completeness, technical challenge attempted, modularity)
  • Problem 4: scalability with number of applicants obviously goes away
  • Problem 5: separating the candidate’s work from the AI’s can be addressed by the live walk-through/augmentation, or by clever honeypotting such that candidates who don’t read the starter code thoroughly invariably produce some artifact

At the end of this process, you’d have a pretty rich set of signals to work from:

  1. Impressiveness of demo
  2. How “deep” did they go on the problem - correlates strongly with interest in company
  3. How much they understand their own code
  4. How well did they do on the DS&A augmentation
  5. Subjective code quality as evaluated by o3

You’d also actually be evaluating along the lines of skills that actually matter for engineers in this day and age. It’s probably legitimately more important to judge how effectively a potential engineering hire can utilize AI dev tools than whether they can implement an LRU cache from scratch. If a candidate is too busy or doesn’t have high enough agency to spend an hour or two working on a genuinely cool technical problem, chances are they wouldn’t be a great hire anyway.

The main drawback to this approach is that administering it would take a lot of ad hoc manpower, er, intelligence. Fortunately, we live in an era where intelligence is rapidly becoming a lot less scarce ;)
