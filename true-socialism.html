True* socialism has never been tried

Jasper Gilley
San Francisco, California
June 2026

I recently found myself describing myself as a believer in both socialism and the Efficient Market Hypothesis, and wanted to briefly elucidate why.

Few sentences get dunked on more reliably than “True* socialism has never been tried.” It's the canonical unfalsifiable cope: every implementation that fails gets retroactively reclassified as not-really-socialism, so the ideal remains permanently unblemished by the evidence. I want to argue that the sentence is nevertheless true — just not in the way the people saying it usually mean. True* socialism has never been tried because nobody has ever been able to afford it.

Socialism is a consumption good

Strip away the theory of history and the labor theory of value, and the operational content of socialism is a purchasing decision: the state buys healthcare, housing, education, pensions, and income security for everyone. This is the most expensive product a civilization can attempt to buy. The money has to come from somewhere, and the only somewhere available is the surplus of the productive sector.

This is the accounting problem that twentieth-century socialism never solved, and mostly never acknowledged. Russia in 1917 had a GDP per capita somewhere around a quarter of America's, with the large majority of its population in subsistence agriculture. The Bolsheviks proposed to fund universal provision off this tax base — and then, for good measure, nationalized the productive sector, which is to say they destroyed the only thing that could conceivably have funded the program. The grain requisitions at gunpoint that followed weren't an unfortunate deviation from an otherwise sound plan. They were the financing mechanism. A state that cannot raise revenue from surplus will extract it from principal, and extracting from principal is what coercion is for.

You can only point guns at so much GDP

The deeper problem is that coercive extraction has a ceiling, and it's much lower than the twentieth century's planners assumed:

  • Evasion: the Soviet “second economy” — off-books production, barter, bribery — was estimated at 10–20% of GDP by the late period. The more you confiscate, the more activity migrates to where you can't see it.
  • Exit: East Germany lost roughly a sixth of its population to emigration before 1961. The Berlin Wall is best understood as capital controls for human beings — an admission that the regime's effective tax rate exceeded what its subjects would pay voluntarily by exactly the width of a wall.
  • Destruction: during collectivization, Soviet peasants slaughtered roughly half the country's livestock rather than hand it over. People don't keep producing things that get confiscated. Deadweight loss isn't a triangle on a chalkboard; it's dead cattle.
  • Enforcement overhead: an apparatus capable of extracting surplus at gunpoint is itself fantastically expensive, and it consumes the very revenue it collects.

Each unit of coercion shrinks the base it's applied to. Coercive state capacity is governed by a Laffer curve that bites early and hard, and the Soviet Union spent seventy years pinned against itYes, the USSR had other problems too. This one was load-bearing. — a state that looked terrifyingly strong but was, fiscally speaking, weak, because its only instrument was one that destroys its own tax base as it operates.

Legitimacy is the state's cost of capital

Now consider what a state can raise when its citizens actually believe in it. During World War II, the US government raised over $185 billion from war bonds — 85 million individual purchasers, in a country of 140 million people, voluntarily lending the state more than half the direct cost of the largest war ever fought. Or consider tax morale today: Sweden and Greece have broadly comparable statutory tax rates and wildly different rates of evasion, and the difference is not enforcement budgets. Swedes, more or less, believe the Swedish state is a good product. Greeks, with some historical justification, don't.

“Voluntary” here doesn't mean taxes become optional. It means the consent of the governed is a continuous variable rather than a binary, and that variable sets the state's effective cost of revenue. A coercive state pays for every marginal dollar with enforcement — auditors, police, walls — and those costs scale linearly at best. A legitimate state raises money the way a good startup raises a Series B: people hand it over because they believe in the roadmap. Legitimacy is, quite literally, the state's cost of capital, and like all costs of capital it compounds.

(The Georgists, who are used to being right about things while getting dunked on, noticed long ago that some taxes are structurally more voluntary than others: a land value tax can't be evaded, exited, or destroyed in protest, because land doesn't go anywhere. It remains the closest thing to coercion-free revenue that public finance has ever devised, which is presumably why almost nobody uses it.)

Indeed, you can view the point of democracy as being not "here's a system for producing good governance decisions" but rather "here's a system for manufacturing political legitimacy". Clearly, democracy produces disastrous policy decisions from time to time. But you can make the case that it's worth it on net if the state has more money with which to finance the services it provides.

It follows that True Socialism has two prerequisites. You need a private sector productive enough to fund universal provision out of surplus rather than principal, and you need a state legitimate enough that the funding is quasi-voluntary, because the coercive alternative caps out long before universal provision gets paid for.

I'll give you two examples for what this can look like, which are really closer to the same example viewed slightly differently. The obvious one is the Nordic states: productive capitalist economies attached to high-legitimacy states, which have historically been able to buy socialist outcomes with surprisingly little enforcement. The other example is one I've experienced directly: wealthy US suburban exclaves like Palo Alto CA, Winnetka IL, Long Island NY, etc. Places like this are simultaneously voluntary real-life-socialist states where you move for a few years so your kids can get an adequately-funded education, as well as urban tax shelters from which you can still commute into the central city without your tax dollars needing to be watered down subsidizing the provision of goods and services for persona non grata.

The reason these are two sides of the same coin, of course, is that the Nordic states and suburban exclaves have historically both funded their welfare states on the basis of exclusion. Both have historically followed a country club model of legitimacy whereby you pay a lot of money to get goods and services as long as you know your money is going to the in-group. In the case of suburban exclaves, the in-group is "other people who are wealthy enough to buy a house here." In the case of European exclaves, the in-group was historically "my ethnic group."

To be honest, this is why I want the United States of America as a concept to work out.

The issue

I've argued before that AI is in the process of doing to cognitive work what the Haber-Bosch process did to farming — a ~20x productivity multiple that converted two-thirds of humanity's employment into 2%. If that's even directionally correct, the first prerequisite is about to be satisfied for the first time in history: a private sector productive enough that universal provision becomes a rounding error on surplus rather than a raid on principal.

The second prerequisite is more likely to be the true limiting reactant long-term. A state that funds redistribution out of AI surplus seized from resentful capital holders, administered by institutions nobody trusts, is just the old failure mode with better technology. The binding constraint on socialism is quietly shifting from productivity to legitimacy — from whether the money exists to whether anyone trusts the state enough to hand it over. Building that legitimacy is a harder engineering problem than the AI was.
